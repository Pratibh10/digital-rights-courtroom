⚖️ Digital Rights Courtroom

A browser-based litigation simulator for EU digital law education, developed for Prof. Eisenberger's course at the University of Vienna.

What Is This?

The Digital Rights Courtroom places students in the role of a lawyer litigating cases under four EU digital regulation frameworks. Each case presents a realistic scenario heard before a regulatory authority or court, with the student representing the complainant against a respondent.

Students progress through five phases per case: Briefing (understand the facts), Investigation (review evidence documents and answer a framework identification question), Witness Examination (select three questions to put to a hostile witness), Courtroom Arguments (identify the legal issue and select the strongest argument for each round), and Verdict (receive a score and outcome with a model answer).

Regulatory Frameworks

The game covers four EU regulatory instruments:

  • AI Act EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) — 34 cases covering prohibited practices (Art. 5), high-risk system requirements (Arts. 9–15), transparency obligations (Art. 50), and GPAI model duties (Art. 53).
  • GDPR General Data Protection Regulation (2016/679) — 14 cases focused on automated decision-making (Art. 22), the right to explanation (Arts. 13–15), consent requirements, and the tension between algorithmic efficiency and individual rights.
  • DSA Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065) — 16 cases covering content moderation (Arts. 16–17), dark patterns (Art. 25), recommender transparency (Art. 27), minor protection (Art. 28), VLOP systemic risk obligations (Arts. 34–35), and researcher data access (Art. 40).
  • DMA Digital Markets Act (Regulation 2022/1925) — 12 cases on gatekeeper obligations including self-preferencing (Art. 6(5)), interoperability (Art. 6(7)), sideloading (Art. 6(4)), anti-steering (Art. 5(4)), data portability (Art. 6(9)), and cross-service data combination (Art. 5(2)).

Case Design Methodology

Each case is built around a plausible-trap architecture. In the courtroom phase, students choose between four argument options of similar length and legal tone:

  • Strong: The correct legal argument citing the right provision with proper application to the facts.
  • Plausible: A sophisticated-sounding argument containing one specific legal error — typically invoking the wrong framework, the wrong article within the right framework, or accepting a defence that should be rejected. This is the designed trap for students with surface-level knowledge.
  • Weak: An argument that identifies the right problem intuitively but fails to cite specific legal provisions or engage with the statutory test.
  • Wrong: A fundamentally flawed argument based on a misunderstanding of the legal framework.

Before selecting an argument, students must first identify the core legal issue in each round (“Spot the Issue”) — a single-click question that tests whether they have correctly framed the problem before seeing the answer choices.

Case scenarios are inspired by real enforcement actions, CJEU preliminary rulings, and DPA decisions across EU Member States. All names, companies, and specific facts are fictionalised.

Scoring System

PhaseWeightHow Points Are Earned
Evidence Review10 ptsProportional to documents reviewed (all 6 = full marks). Includes a framework identification MCQ on cases that have one.
Witness Examination25 ptsBased on question selection from 5 options (choose 3): effective questions score highest; risky questions may backfire; ineffective questions score lowest.
Courtroom Arguments65 ptsQuality of selected argument (strong = full, weak = half, plausible/wrong = zero) × citation accuracy (correct evidence = 1.0×, wrong evidence = 0.5×).

Verdicts: Won (≥75), Won with Reservations (≥50), Lost (≥25), Dismissed (<25).

Witness Examination Design

Each case presents 5 possible questions to put to the witness, but students may only select 3. Questions are displayed simultaneously in randomised order so position does not indicate quality. After selecting 3 and submitting, all three exchanges play out with feedback on why each question was effective, risky, or ineffective.

  • Effective (typically 3 available): Use specific evidence to extract damaging admissions that advance the legal argument directly.
  • Risky (typically 1 available): Give the witness an opportunity to deliver a compelling defence. May sound reasonable but hands the witness control of the narrative.
  • Ineffective (typically 1 available): Address peripheral matters that do not establish a legal violation, wasting a limited question slot.

Legal Codex

The sidebar Legal Codex provides quick reference to key articles from all four regulatory frameworks. Each entry includes the official regulatory text alongside a plain-language summary. The codex highlights articles relevant to the current case. Students are encouraged to use it during gameplay, just as a practitioner would consult the statute during proceedings.

For Instructors

Student scores are collected automatically via a cloud database (Supabase). No student action is required — scores are recorded the moment a case is completed. Instructors can view all student performance data, filter by class, and export results as CSV from the Supabase dashboard.

Sessions are managed per class: the instructor can start and end sessions, and change access codes, directly from the dashboard without any developer involvement.

The feedback button (📨 in the bottom-right corner) is available throughout gameplay. Students can flag legal errors, report confusing content, or submit suggestions. All submissions include the student's name, class, case number, and screen context.

Technical Details

The Digital Rights Courtroom is a client-side application built with vanilla JavaScript, HTML, and CSS, deployed as a static site on GitHub Pages. Student scores and feedback are stored in a Supabase PostgreSQL database. Class configuration (active sessions and access codes) is managed through the Supabase dashboard. No server-side framework is used.
